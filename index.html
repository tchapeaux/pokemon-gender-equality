Gender pay gap in the Pokémon video games

Context

The Pokémon games have been an international success for more than 20 years, with console games attracting hundreds of millions of players, a mobile app that became a global phenomenon, and a Hollywood movie.

In the video game narrative, the Player embodies a Pokémon trainer, who collects and trains many types of creatures. The main mechanic of the games is to fight other trainers, until the Player is crowned Pokémon Champion. After each victory, the Player is awarded a bit of prize money from the defeated challenger. On the contrary, if the Player is defeated, they lose some prize money, the amount of which is exactly half their current money .

Methodology

Our goal is to use the prize money system of the Pokemon games to estimate the gender pay gap between men and women in the Pokémon world.

Interestingly, being a Pokémon trainer does not seem to be a full-time job, but rather a hobby enjoyed by almost anyone, be it scientists, police agents or politicians. Because the prize money amount is equal to half the current wealth of the defeated party, we suppose that the prize money amount is proportional to that person's salary, and analyze the pay gap directly from there.

This analysis is based on the trainers encountered during the first generation of Pokémon games, released in 1996. Note that subsequent re-releases of the games include more gender diversity in the trainers.

Full-screen graphs (without comments)

Gender gap in the trainer encounters

This first graph is not concerned with the prize money yet. We just count how many men and women trainers the Player has the opportunity to fight during a normal playthrough of the game.

In the end, the player encounters 323 trainers, and only 77 of them are women.

Prize money gender gap through the game

In this graph, we count the total amount of money received by the player as prize money by men and women during the game.

The graph is somewhat similar to the number of encounters, which seems consistent. However, the high difference between the number of men and women makes it difficult to compare the absolute value of prize money received by each gender.

Average prize money

To get a clearer picture, we look at the average value of the prize money after each encounter.

Even if the lack of diversity in trainers should be seen in itself as a problem, this analysis was concerned with the perceived wealth of each gender.

From that point of view, the Pokémon world is surpringly gender-balanced. Both gender pay similar amounts of prize money during the game, with the women even paying 7% more (on average) than men at the end of the game. This means that men from the Pokémon world could argue that they work for free 26 days per year.

More realistically, this difference is probably due to the very low number of female trainers, meaning that the women from the Gym Leaders and Elite Four subgroups (who acts as mini-bosses during the games with higher prize money) have a higher weight on the results.
